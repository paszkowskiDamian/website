A design system doesn't fail at the component level. Buttons are easy. It fails in the seams — the moment two products need the same component to do slightly different things, and nobody agreed in advance on who bends.

## Tokens before components

The layer that actually scales is the one with no opinions about UI: color, type, spacing, radii, expressed as named decisions. Components churn with product needs; the token contract can survive a full visual redesign.

1. Name tokens for their role, never their value — `accent`, not `red-600`.
2. Let every platform consume the same source of truth, even if the format differs.
3. Version the tokens like an API, because that's what they are.

## The system is a product, with users

Adoption is won in the gaps between office hours: documentation that answers the question at 2am, changelogs that explain why, escape hatches that don't feel like punishment. Teams route around a system that says "no" too often — a good one makes the right thing the lazy thing.

<PullQuote
  quote="Every abstraction you expose is a promise you keep for years."

/>

Ship the smallest system that two products actually share, and grow it only along the lines where they keep agreeing. That's not a compromise — that's the system finding its true shape.
